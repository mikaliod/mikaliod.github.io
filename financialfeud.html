Financial Feud

A Bank Service Promoting Financial Literacy

How can banks help promote teenagers' financial literacy? PNC Bank reached out to us in search of a service design to answer that question. The solution by our team is an application-embedded service that provides families with weekly financial challenges, which encourages teens to build basic financial skills while building a relationship with PNC. This is a class project for Service Design.

Domain Research

To understand the domain better, we did research on the PNC Financial Literacy Program, and conducted competitive analysis and surveys with teenagers, young adults, and parents. The researches led to several key findings:

  1. Managing finances is usually initiated by a trigger.
  2. Finance is seen as a personal issue, but it can be a community/family topic.
  3. Programs promoting financial literacy should be tailored to an individual’s knowledge, ability, demographics, life stage, and learning styles.
  4. Most successful applications maintain engagement with people by giving continuous, immediate, and unambiguous feedback.

Stakeholder Analysis

We started the stakeholder analysis by brainstorming and  listing out all of the stakeholders.

We discovered from further analysis that, when it comes to finances:

  1. The influence on the parents and teens are similar.
  2. Parents have a strong and direct influence on teens.
  3. Banks' influence is only on the parents.

This led us to redesign the stakeholder map in a way that showed these findings more clearly. And we decided to focus more on exploring how we can take advantage of the parents' influences on teens to make up for the lack of engagement with the teens from the banks.

Problem Statement & Opportunity Area

The problem is defined to be that, PNC Bank does not have a direct influence to teens, and they want to engage with these teens by helping to promote their financial literacy.

The opportunity area we identified is that PNC can leverage the parent-teen relationship and provide a financial service to the teens and the whole family.

Ideation

Starting off from the opportunity area, we began ideating how to solve the problem.

We first made three personae, each representing an age group.

Based on the persona and scenarios, we then brainstormed and narrowed down the possible solution down to five. Storyboards were created for each, and the value added to PNC Bank was also analyzed. You can find detailed info from page 23 in the process book. We then tested these ideas with people by speed dating.

From the speed dating, we received the most positive reactions for the 30 day challenge application, which presents financial challenges for families to complete. However, users wanted to modify the challenges to make the time period shorter for each. We also found that teens wanted greater incentives to invest time into the challenges. This leaded us to improve the idea by reducing the challenges timespan and creating rewards that drive engagement. We then began designing the application based upon this idea.

solution

The solution is Financial Feud, an application that provides families with weekly financial challenges.

 

Every week the application focus on a different financial domain and assign each family member a task to complete. The challenges are designed to create accountability and competitive spirit between family members.

 

These tasks encourage teens to build basic financial skills while building a relationship with PNC Bank.

future steps

In the future we see this application being used to build community financial literacy as well. This can be shared by teens with their friends and they can have group challenges. Similar concepts can be applied to the parents too.

This also helps in building the credibility of PNC Bank where more communities will vouch for them and engage in their services.
