Financial Feud

service design · finance · mobile app

How can banks help improve teenagers' financial literacy?

Challenge

Outcome

Reports show that more than 20% of United States teenage students lack basic financial literacy skills. In a world full of digital and global financial activity, the potential for financial scams, and the upcoming long-term life decisions involving money, this lack of knowledge can be damaging. Because of this, PNC Bank wanted us to design a service that can help improve teenagers' financial literacy.

My team and I designed an application-embedded service that provides families with weekly financial challenges, and it encourages teens to build basic financial skills with their family together, while building a relationship with PNC.

Role

Teammates

My teammates and I worked together on generative research, ideation, evaluative research. Individually, I was responsible for prototyping the mobile application and making mock-ups.

Aishwarya Dwivedi, Tiffany Lai, Xuejiao Liu, Reva Pabba

Design Overview

The mobile application provides financial challenges for the family to engage in. It also analyzes family's data and recommends more challenges that are tailored to the family.

Challenges involve tasks to be completed by each family member, which creates opportunities for teenagers to learn financial knowledge through first-hand activities.

Upon finishing a challenge, virtual rewards are given. These rewards can be turned into discount coupons or monetary prizes, which are transferred from the parents' bank account and stored in a card for the teenagers to use.

Design Process

Generative Research

We started the design process by doing a stakeholder analysis, brainstorming and listing out all of what is involved in a teenager's life. We also listed out influences on parents, since they are closely related to teens.

We found that when it comes to finances, though a lot of influence on the parents and teens are similar, Banks' influence is only on the parents. This led us to move research direction towards exploring the parent-teen relationship in terms of finance.

Stakeholder interviews

Literature reviews

We interviewed representatives from PNC Bank about their established effort in financial literacy, the resources they have to offer, and their vision of what can be promising directions.

We reviewed 10 papers whose topics range from the current state of financial literacy among teenagers to evaluation of financial education programs / mobile applications.

Competitive analysis

Online surveys

We analyzed 7 other products on the market whose purposes are to improve financial literacy among teenagers and students, including CMU Student Financial Services, Bank of America, Capital One, ThreeJars, etc.

We set up an online survey to young students in college asking them about their financial behavior, and another survey to parents inquiring about their involvement in their children's financial education.

Literature reviews

Competitive analysis

Online surveys

These generative researches led to several key findings:

Finance is seen as a personal issue, but it can also be a community/family topic.

Managing finances is usually initiated by a trigger.

Programs promoting financial literacy should be tailored to an individual’s knowledge, ability, demographics, life stage, and learning styles.

Most successful applications maintain engagement with people by giving continuous, immediate, and unambiguous feedback.

Problem Framing

Problem statement

Opportunity area

Though PNC want to engage with these teens by helping to promote their financial literacy, it does not have a direct influence to teens.

PNC can leverage the parent-teen relationship and provide a financial service to the teens and the whole family, which would be personalized and provide continuous trigger.

Starting off from the opportunity area, we began ideating how to solve the problem.

Around these personae, we brainstormed 30+ solutions about how PNC can help them and solve their frustrations, and narrowed down to 5 top solutions that we think would work the best based on our research findings. We created storyboards for each, and tested these ideas with teens and parents through speed-dating sessions to validate if they share the same pain points with our personae and their thoughts on our ideas.

From the speed-dating sessions, we received the most positive reactions for the 30-day challenge idea, where an application would present financial challenges for families to complete. We improved the idea from teenagers and parents' feedback, including reducing the challenges timespan, creating a reward system that would drive engagement, etc. We then began designing the application based upon this idea.

Solution

The solution is Financial Feud, an application-embedded service that provides families with weekly financial challenges. These challenges creates opportunities for teenagers to learn financial knowledge through first-hand activities. Upon finishing a challenge, virtual rewards are given that can be turned into monetary prizes.

Various challenges to choose from

From our research we found that people have various financial goals they want to achieve. Financial Feud provides families with a wide range of financial challenges in categories such as budgeting, investment, credit, etc. to meet their own needs.

Tailoring challenges to each family

Our research also showed that financial education programs work the best when they are tailored to an individual’s knowledge, demographics, life stage, etc. When granted permission from the families, Financial Feud uses data provided by the family or from their bank accounts to recommend challenges that best suit the family.

Reward system

Another research finding is that managing finances is usually initiated by a trigger or an incentive, especially for teenagers. Financial Feud has a reward system that gives family rewards upon finishing challenges. These rewards can be redeemed for monetary prizes or coupons. Families can apply for a card which allows the teenagers to use these prizes directly.

Value model

This service creates value for not only the families but also PNC as well. On one hand, Financial Feud helps not only teenagers but also other people in a family become more financially responsible. On the other hand, the service provides PNC with data, such as what financial domain are customers most interested in. It engages teenagers, who will become PNC's potential client. The service also encourage family to engage in other PNC services such as opening up savings accounts.
