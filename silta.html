Silta

mobility · system design

How could future mobility help bridge communities?

Challenge

Outcome

Pittsburgh is growing at a fast pace with the increase number in students and young working professionals coming into the city. This influx of individuals, however, is creating a social division between the old and the new, causing issues such as gentrification in many of Pittsburgh’s neighborhoods. Our team worked with Bridgestone to ideate how this problem can be solved through future technology in mobility.

Our team designed a vehicle with adjustable and expandable walls. These vehicles can form assemblies and create event spaces in city neighborhoods that facilitate social interactions between communities.

Teammates

Design Overview

Silta is an automobile system with vehicles that create event spaces in city neighborhoods to facilitate social interactions between communities.

Similar to food trucks and pop-up farmer’s markets, Silta moves temporary community spaces around local neighborhoods. Each Silta unit is operated autonomous driving, and is as large as an average bus which fits perfectly into current infrastructure.

Expandable surfaces - each unit can be fully opened to create dynamic spaces for different events

Miura folding - significantly increase area of space the vehicles create while maintaining the compact nature of the units

Walls with controllable rigidity - soft and flexible to be folded in the miura form, but rigid when in the expanded form to support people and other items (inspired by jamSheets)

Omni wheels - sub-wheels enabling alternative traveling directions ensure accurate and efficient assembly of the units

Phase I · Background Research

We started our research by conducting secondary research on three main areas:

The domain of mobility

The company of Bridgestone

We wanted to study trends in future mobility through literature reviews. Four trends we identified are: shift from car ownership to mobility as service, shared economy, automated driving, and electrification.

We wanted to understand Bridgestone’s competence and focus areas. Aside from shared economy and autonomous vehicle, which we already saw during literature review, two other interesting focus areas we found were material innovation and AI & big data.

This round of research gave us an overview of what future mobility technology we could use as tools to solve problems.

Phase II · Exploratory Research

As we saw the differences in Pittsburgh neighborhoods, during the second phase of research, our focus was to explore how people and communities can be connected through emerging technologies in mobility. We had two main research activities.

Interactive booklet

We initially targeted our focus on bridging “the old” and “the new” of Pittsburgh, so we conducted interviews with newcomers and local residents to understand their lifestyle regarding mobility and views of Pittsburgh. We designed two interactive booklets for the interviewees, with activities include:

For newcomers:

  • Draw a map of Pittsburgh & one of your neighborhood.
  • What does your average weekend look like?
  • How do you normally travel in Pittsburgh?
  • Draw your journey as newcomer to Pittsburgh.
  • ...

For local residents:

  • Draw a map of Pittsburgh & one of your neighborhood.
  • How has your neighborhood changed over time?
  • At what point did you no longer consider yourself a newcomer?
  • ...

Interactive poster

We also conducted an open-space user study with an interactive poster to see how people connect with other neighborhoods in Pittsburgh. We asked the following two questions:

  • Where do you live? Work? Frequently hang out? Want to explore?
  • What stops you from exploring Pittsburgh?

After studying results from the interactive booklets and the poster, our key findings were:

  • People define their world of life based on experiences rather than geography.
  • People don't spend much time exploring other Pittsburgh neighborhoods.
  • People prefer to explore new neighborhoods with social company (friends, family, etc.).
  • People's main motivations to going out are food and entertainment.
  • People are impeded by time & schedule, transportation constraints, and budget.

Problem Statement

People in Pittsburgh live within small physical and social radii, which is isolating them from other communities and gradually ripping apart the social fabric of society.

Ideation

Brainstorming

To solve the problem we identified, we brainstormed multiple ideas using methodologies such as Things from the Future exercise. These ideas include a digital solution called “Pittsburgh Passport”, an one-person vehicle that can be assembled into groups, etc.

Changing perspective

After reviewing our ideas with Bridgestone and our instructor, we realized that the concepts lacked innovation and wouldn't solve the problem fundamentally.

Inspired by the feedback from stakeholders, we altered our perspective and changed our goal from “motivating and bringing people to places” to “bringing experiences to people”.

We ideated the final solution to be a vehicle that would operate in a way similar to food trucks and bring experiences from one neighborhood to another.
